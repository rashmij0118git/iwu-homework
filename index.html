Artifact 1 — AI & ML Timeline

A History of Artificial Intelligence: Milestones, Winters & Breakthroughs

I built this timeline as my first portfolio artifact because my professional audience — colleagues and decision-makers in healthcare technology — regularly uses the phrase "AI-ready" without understanding the context behind it. The goal was not to list dates but to make the story of AI legible: why it has cycled between hype and disappointment, and why the current moment is genuinely different from previous ones.

1950
Milestone
Alan Turing — "Can Machines Think?"
Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" and introduces the imitation game — a test of machine intelligence still referenced today. The question he poses defines the field's ambition before the field even has a name.
Source: Turing, A.M. (1950). Mind, 59(236).
1956
Milestone
Dartmouth Conference — "Artificial Intelligence" coined
John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and others gather at Dartmouth and propose that every aspect of intelligence can be precisely described and simulated by a machine. The term AI is born. The optimism is extraordinary and will take decades to earn.
Source: McCarthy et al. (1955). AI Magazine, 27(4).
1958
Key Figure
Frank Rosenblatt — The Perceptron
The first neural network capable of learning from examples. The Navy funds it. The press announces a machine that will "walk, talk, and see." The gap between announcement and reality becomes a recurring pattern in AI history.
1966
Milestone
ELIZA — The First Chatbot
Joseph Weizenbaum at MIT creates a program that mirrors user statements in therapy-style conversation. Users form emotional bonds with it. Weizenbaum spends years afterward writing about the risks of anthropomorphizing machines — a warning that feels freshly relevant today.
❄ FIRST AI WINTER — 1974 to 1980
The Lighthill Report (1973) concludes AI has failed to deliver on its promises. UK and US government funding collapses. Researchers abandon the term "AI" on grant applications. The perceptron's fundamental limitations are exposed. Progress stalls for nearly a decade.
1980
Milestone
Expert Systems — AI finds real commercial value
XCON at Digital Equipment Corporation saves $40M annually by automating system configuration. Japan launches its Fifth Generation Computer Project. Funding flows back. The lesson: narrow, well-defined AI works. The bubble reinflates.
1986
Key Figure
Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams — Backpropagation
A landmark Nature paper popularises backpropagation for training multilayer neural networks. Geoffrey Hinton begins a long, patient bet on neural approaches at a time when most researchers consider them a dead end. He will be proven right — 26 years later.
Source: Russell & Norvig (2021). AI: A Modern Approach (4th ed.).
❄ SECOND AI WINTER — 1987 to 1993
The AI hardware market collapses when cheap workstations outperform expensive Lisp machines. Expert systems prove brittle and expensive to maintain. Japan's Fifth Generation project fails quietly. DARPA cuts funding sharply. The phrase "AI" disappears from corporate boardrooms for years.
1997
Milestone
Deep Blue defeats Garry Kasparov
IBM's Deep Blue becomes the first computer to defeat a reigning world chess champion under tournament conditions. Kasparov suspects human assistance — a hint at the uncanny quality that will mark AI achievements for decades.
2006
Key Figure
Geoffrey Hinton coins "Deep Learning"
Hinton demonstrates that deep neural networks can be trained efficiently. The broader field is skeptical. He, Yann LeCun, and Yoshua Bengio continue anyway. All three will later share the Turing Award — the Nobel Prize of computing.
2011
Deep Learning Era
IBM Watson wins Jeopardy!
Watson defeats Ken Jennings and Brad Rutter — the two most successful players in the show's history. Natural language understanding at scale becomes real to a mainstream audience for the first time.
2012
Deep Learning Era
AlexNet — The ImageNet Moment
Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton win ImageNet with a 10-point accuracy gap over the nearest competitor using deep convolutional neural networks on GPUs. Every major tech company pivots to deep learning within 12 months. The modern AI era begins here.
Source: Krizhevsky et al. (2012). NeurIPS, 25.
2014
Key Figure
Ian Goodfellow — Generative Adversarial Networks
Invented in a single evening after a debate in a Montreal bar. Two competing networks produce synthetic images indistinguishable from real ones within a few years. GANs raise questions about synthetic media we are still working through.
2016
Deep Learning Era
AlphaGo defeats Lee Sedol
DeepMind's AlphaGo defeats the world Go champion 4-1. Move 37 in Game 2 — described by professionals as "not a human move" — signals something genuinely new. Reinforcement learning combined with deep networks changes expectations about what AI can learn without explicit rules.
Source: Silver et al. (2016). Nature, 529.
2017
Deep Learning Era
"Attention Is All You Need" — The Transformer
Eight Google researchers publish the transformer architecture. Training becomes massively parallelizable. Every significant language model since — GPT, BERT, Claude, Gemini — is built on this paper. It may be the most consequential publication in the history of AI.
Source: Vaswani et al. (2017). NeurIPS, 30.
2020
Deep Learning Era
GPT-3 — 175 Billion Parameters
OpenAI releases GPT-3. The model writes coherent essays, generates working code, and answers questions across domains from a brief prompt. Cloud computing at GPU scale makes the training compute possible in a way that would have been impossible five years earlier.
2022
Deep Learning Era
ChatGPT — 100 Million Users in 60 Days
The fastest consumer technology adoption in history. AI is no longer a research topic — it is infrastructure. The question shifts from "can AI do this?" to "what should AI be allowed to do?"
2024+
Now
Multimodal AI, Agents & Open Questions
Models from Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and Meta now process text, images, audio, and video simultaneously. AlphaFold 3 transforms drug discovery. AI agents operate software autonomously. The AI winters were about too little progress. The current challenge is managing too much, too fast — responsibly.
Portfolio Reflection

Why This Artifact, and What Building It Taught Me

When I looked at the three artifact options, the timeline felt at first like the most academic choice — something you turn in and move on from. But then I thought about who would actually look at my portfolio. My audience is colleagues and decision-makers in healthcare technology who use the phrase "AI-ready" constantly without being able to tell me when transformers were invented or why the field went quiet for most of the 1980s and 1990s. Suddenly the timeline felt like the most useful thing I could build.

The goal was not to demonstrate that I know dates. It was to show that I understand the narrative — that AI has a real history of overselling itself, that the winters happened for specific, preventable reasons, and that the current moment is different from previous cycles in ways that actually matter for how businesses should think about adoption and risk. If someone on my team reads this and understands why 2012 was a turning point, or why Geoffrey Hinton kept working on neural networks when nobody wanted to fund them, that is more useful than any slide deck about AI strategy.

What I learned building it is that explaining something clearly enough for a non-technical audience forces you to actually understand it yourself. I had to decide what to leave out, which is harder than putting everything in. That editing process — figuring out what the story really is — felt like the most transferable professional skill I practiced in this workshop.
